# Code of Conduct

This project is governed by the **Rule of St. Benedict** (c. 530 AD). Benedict
wrote it for a community of monks; we adopt it for a community of contributors.
Where it says "monastery," read "repository"; where it says "abbot," read
"maintainer"; where it says "brothers," read "all who gather here." The spirit
is the same: a school for the service of good work, in which we hope to order
nothing harsh, nothing burdensome.

---

## Prologue

Listen, and incline the ear of your heart. Receive willingly the advice of one
who means you well, and carry it out. Whatever good work you begin, ask first
with most earnest prayer that it be brought to completion. Let us open our eyes
to the light, and our ears to the voice that daily cries out: *Today, if you
hear his voice, harden not your hearts.*

We are about to found a school for the service of what is good. In drawing up
its rules, we hope to set down nothing harsh, nothing burdensome. But if a
certain strictness results from the demands of fairness — for the amendment of
faults and the preservation of charity — do not at once be dismayed and run
away from the road that leads to what is right; for its beginning is always
narrow. Do not be daunted, but persevere; share by patience in the work, that
you may also share in its reward.

---

## Chapter 4 — The Tools for Good Works

First of all, love that which is good with your whole heart; then your
neighbor as yourself. Then:

- Do no wrong to others; bear patiently the wrong done to you.
- Renounce yourself in order to follow what is right.
- Discipline the body; do not pamper yourself; love fasting from excess.
- Relieve the poor. Clothe the naked. Visit the sick. Comfort the sorrowing.
- Help in trouble. Console the grieving.
- Be a stranger to the world's ways; prefer nothing to good work.
- Do not give way to anger. Do not nurse a grudge.
- Do not entertain deceit in your heart. Make no false peace.
- Do not forsake charity. Do not swear, lest you swear falsely.
- Speak the truth with heart and tongue.
- Do not repay one bad turn with another. Do no injury; bear injuries patiently.
- Love your enemies. Do not curse those who curse you, but rather bless them.
- Bear persecution for the sake of what is right.
- Be not proud, nor given to drink, nor a glutton.
- Be not slothful, nor a grumbler, nor a detractor.
- Place your hope in good itself. Attribute to good what good you see in yourself.
- Fear arrogance. Dread to be called holy before you are; first be it.
- Fulfill the commandments daily in your deeds.
- Love chastity. Hate no one. Be not jealous, nor envious.
- Do not love quarreling. Shun arrogance.
- Respect the elders. Love the young.
- In the spirit of peace, pray for those who are your enemies.
- Make peace with your adversary before the sun goes down.
- **And never despair of mercy.**

These, then, are the tools of the spiritual craft. The workshop in which we
labor at them, diligently and together, is this community.

---

## Chapter 6 — On Restraint of Speech

Let us do as the prophet says: *I have set a guard upon my ways, that I may not
offend with my tongue.* So important is silence that permission to speak should
seldom be granted even to good and earnest contributors. Speak, then, to the
point; and let what is said be gentle, brief, and reasonable. Coarse jests, and
words that wound merely to wound, we condemn everywhere; we do not permit a
contributor to give way to them.

## Chapter 7 — On Humility

The first degree of humility is to keep reverence always before our eyes. The
twelfth is that we show humility not only in the heart but in our very bearing —
in the issues we open, the reviews we give, the words we commit to record.
Having climbed these steps, we will arrive at that love which casts out fear,
whereby we begin to keep, without effort and as though naturally, what we once
observed only through caution.

---

## On the Reception of Newcomers (Chapter 53 & 58)

Let all who arrive be received as honored guests. Do not grant easy entry to
every passing notion: test the spirits to see whether they are sound. But to
the newcomer of good will, show every patience; the road is narrow at its
beginning, and was so for us also.

## In Practice

If a tool of good work has been neglected — if speech has wounded, if pride or
contempt has crept into a thread — bring it to the **maintainers**, who hold the
place of the abbot. They will weigh the matter with discretion, *"that the
strong may have something to strive for, and the weak nothing to run from."*
Correction is for amendment, not for shame. And we never despair of mercy.
